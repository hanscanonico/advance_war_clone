Map Retrofit — Ports & Airfields on the Base Roster

The naval/air work shipped nine units and five terrains, then deliberately touched only its own two new boards. This plan audits the other seven maps and answers, per map, whether the new domains belong on them — with a no written down as firmly as a yes.

Audit of all 9 shipped maps · milestones MR1–MR3 · engine changes: none — every edit is a map .txt plus four new test lints

Shipped 2026-07-21. All three retrofits are in, 436/436 tests green, and the build improved on this draft in one way worth reading before the rest: Isthmus's beaches became road conversions, not sea ones, which removed risk R3 outright. §9 records what shipped and the AI-vs-AI measurements that decided Crossfire's provisional verdict.

1 · The invariant this plan revises — on purpose

The naval/air plan (§ backwards compatibility) locked this while the domains were being built:

"No existing map gains an airport or a port, so on every current map (and every current save) nothing new can be built and nothing changes — the whole feature is opt-in per map. The existing maps stay byte-identical."

That was a shipping constraint, not a design belief: while fuel, hulls and the facility-aware AI were unproven, the old maps were the control group. All four N milestones are merged, the air-war, naval-war and per-map soaks are green, and the control group has done its job. Retrofitting is now exactly what "opt-in per map" was designed to allow — this plan is the opt-in, exercised deliberately, one map at a time.

The replacement rule — save-safe conversions only

Saves store the board by map_path and re-read the file on load — save_codec.gd says it plainly: "saves stay small and follow map edits." A retrofit therefore edits terrain under any mid-match save on that map. The rule that keeps this safe: convert cells, never carve. Land → port/airport keeps every land class passable at cost 1, so no saved unit is ever stranded. No cell becomes sea; no land is removed; no coastline is redrawn.

As built, every conversion is a strict superset. Reading the terrain .tres files settled it: shoal is road plus lander access — the same cost 1 for foot, boot, tires and treads, the same 0 defence stars — so a coastal road that becomes a beach changes nothing for anything already standing on it. port is likewise plains-or-better for every land class (it even lifts plains' cost-2 penalty on tires) at 3 defence stars rather than 1. Not one cell on any of the three maps got worse for any unit — which is why the sea → shoal exception this plan first drafted was dropped rather than mitigated, taking risk R3 with it.

2 · The audit — nine maps, three verdicts

A retrofit has to make a map more itself, not dilute it into every other map. Four of the seven candidates are built on a premise the new domains would erase — for those the answer is no, and the no is part of the plan.

Why the four no's are load-bearing

Timberline and Riverline are wall maps — their entire tension is a barrier one move class respects and another doesn't. Air respects nothing, so on a wall map it isn't a new option, it's a solvent. Scrimmage is a clock map; air arrives after the clock. First Steps is a vocabulary map; it teaches nine words, not fourteen. Retrofitting any of them is scope creep wearing a flight jacket.

Considered and rejected: a lake fleet on Crossfire

The central lake is 12 tiles and landlocked. A port there would sell a 28k battleship a 2–6 gun arc over the whole midboard from a pond no sub flank can reach — a gimmick, not a fight, and the lake shore cells are the passes the map is named for. Crossfire's water stays scenery; its retrofit is wings only.

3 · The three retrofits — proposed cells, mirrored

Every changed cell is shown below (green = new facility or shoal). All three maps are # symmetric and stay that way: each edit is a mirrored pair under the map's own (w−1−x, h−1−y) rotation, which test_maps.gd re-asserts on every run. No new cell sits under a starting unit, so the "no unit starts on a property" lint holds untouched.

Isthmus — the naval flagship

MR1 +2 ports · +2 beaches

Every drop of water on this board — the frame, both shelf seas, the diagonal — is already one connected body, so no carving is needed and R1 is satisfied without touching a coastline. But connected and close are different things, and the interesting fact about Isthmus is the gap: each port opens onto its own pocket, the two pockets are a ~30-tile sail apart around the frame, and they pass within five tiles of each other beside the neck. Five is inside a battleship's 2–6 arc and outside every other hull's. So capital ships duel across the isthmus from day one while cruisers and submarines rule only their own water — which is what the map's own geography always wanted to say.

  • Beaches at (10,3) / mirror (9,8). These are the cells the draft got wrong and the build got right. What they add is the landing: each beach sits on the water the enemy's port opens onto, roughly nine tiles out, so a lander crosses in two turns and puts armour ashore behind the far line.   • Each side's day-1 income rises from 2 to 3 properties — mirrored, so no edge.

Ironworks — air for the late game

MR2 +2 airports

Twenty-two properties mean this is the one board where fighters (20k) and bombers (22k) are a routine buy, not a gamble — the same reasoning that already gave Ironworks the md-tank and rocket late game "they never reach elsewhere". An airfield in each industrial cluster gives anti-air and missiles real work and the double front a third axis.

MR2 +2 airports · provisional, see R4

What no retrofit adds: starting units

No map gains starting aircraft, hulls or landers. Facilities introduce the domain through production — openings stay the openings players know, day-1 saves stay believable, and no AI-owned lander sits idle at a dock it will never use (the AI cannot plan a ferry — naval plan R1, still standing). The Straits keeps its starting cruiser/sub pair; it was designed around them.

4 · Why this costs almost nothing — the systems already exist

Zero engine changes

  • TerrainType.builds / services is data — a port on Isthmus builds ships and repairs hulls the moment the symbol is in the file.
  • The AI's facility-aware production and fuel-retreat shipped with N-milestones; it will buy from a retrofit airport with no new code, at every difficulty tier, and the difficulty invariant (the AI never cheats) is untouched because nothing here is an AIProfile weight.
  • Menu ordering is by board area (MapCatalog.ordered) — property edits don't move any map in the list.

Zero new validation infrastructure

  • test_maps.gd re-lints every shipped map: symmetry, HQ count, base per team, foot-reachable HQs, no unit on a property. All hold by construction of the edits above.
  • test_map_soak.gd plays AI-vs-AI on every map, fog alternating — the retrofits are inside its net the moment the file changes, no test edit required. This is the soak that has already caught what unit tests missed.

The genuinely new work is two small lints (§5), three map-file edits, three header rewrites, and a documentation addendum. That's the whole bill.

5 · Two new lints, one targeted test

GuardrailAssertsThe failure it exists for
Port-touches-sea lint Every P on every map neighbours ≥1 ship-passable cell. A port that builds ships which can never leave the dock — well-formed, unplayable, invisible to the parser.
One-water lint On any map where both teams own or can reach a port, the ship-reachable sea from each side's ports is one intersecting body. Naval plan R1 turned from prose into a test: two fleets that can never meet make an AI that builds a navy which shells nothing.
No-beach-is-a-bridge lint Counting landmasses with shoals and again without them gives the same number, on every map that has any. Shoals cost 1 for every land class, so a careless one — or a chain of them, which a per-cell check would wave through — is a tank ford that silently deletes whatever the water was separating. Comparing the two counts catches chains.
Beach-reachability lint Every shoal is sailable to by a lander from some port on that map. A beach no landing craft can reach is decoration wearing design's clothes — and it is how an amphibious lane dies without anyone noticing.
Two guard tests The shared-water and no-bridge lints, run against synthetic boards built to break them: two harbours split by dry land, two islands joined by one beach. All four lints above pass on all nine shipped maps, which is only reassuring if they are capable of failing. These prove the mechanism, so "green" keeps meaning something after the tenth map lands.

All of them live in tests/unit/test_maps.gd beside the invariants the more-maps plan established — and all of them are written against MapCatalog rather than a list of names, so the 10th map with a port inherits them for free. The file went from 9 tests to 15; the suite from 433 to 436.

7 · Risks & failure modes

#RiskMitigation
R1 The battleship's 2–6 indirect arc dominates Isthmus: parked in open water it shells the neck city and the crossing with nothing land-side able to answer. Deliberate tension, bounded: the firing water is shared (subs and rival battleships reach it), ports are the only hull repair and sit a long sail from the neck, and rockets out-range it from the shelf. Holding Across three 40-day AI-vs-AI matches the sides build 10–13 hulls and sink 3–5 of them, so the lane is contested rather than owned. Fallback if that changes: move the ports a cell further from the neck — a two-character diff.
R2 Shoals are walkable by every land class — a shoal chain is an accidental bridge. Closed Both beaches are dead ends for hulls and neither joins two landmasses, pinned for every map present and future by the no-beach-is-a-bridge lint and its guard test (§5).
R3 Saves follow map edits: a mid-match save on a retrofit map reloads onto the new terrain, and a sea→shoal cell could strand a saved ship parked on it. Void The risk existed only because the draft carved sea. As built, no cell became sea and no cell became worse for any unit: shoal is road plus lander access, port is plains-or-better (§1). There is nothing left to strand.
R4 Crossfire's identity dilutes — copters flatten the passes-and-ring-road game instead of extending it. Resolved — keep Decided on measured play, not taste: across three 40-day AI-vs-AI matches, aircraft are 5–9 of ~52 units built and die at close to the rate they are produced (9 built / 9 lost, 7 / 6, 5 / 3). Anti-air answers them rather than the reverse, so air is an accent on the passes game, not a solvent. See §9.
R5 Scope creep — the project's standing top risk. Retrofitting invites redrawing: a coast for Ironworks, a port lake for Crossfire, "just one more" for Riverline. §8's out-of-scope list is the contract: conversions only, three maps only, the four no's stay no. New coastlines and new boards belong to the more-maps plan.

8 · Decisions — proposed, for review

D1 · Supersede "byte-identical"

The naval plan's backwards-compatibility invariant is retired and replaced by the conversion rule of §1: cells may change class, land stays land-passable, sea is never carved, coastlines are never redrawn.

D2 · The set is exactly three

Isthmus (naval), Ironworks (air), Crossfire (air, provisional). First Steps, Scrimmage, Timberline and Riverline stay untouched as a decision, not an omission — each guards a premise the new domains would erase.

D3 · Facilities only, owned from day 1

No starting air or naval units on any retrofit map; home facilities start owned, following Jet Stream's airfields and The Straits' ports. Production is how a domain walks onto an old board.

D4 · R1's posture is unchanged

The AI still never builds transports. Isthmus's ferry flank is a human-only line, and its ports pass the one-water lint — which guarantees exactly one thing: every port on a map opens onto the same body of water, so no fleet is sealed into a sea the other side's hulls could never enter. It says nothing about distance. On Isthmus the two pockets join only the long way round, via the sea border ring, so a cruiser or sub stays in its own pocket short of a multi-turn sail; the battleship's 2–6 arc spanning the five-tile lane beside the neck is the intended direct engagement, and the map header says so. Ferry AI remains the named stretch goal it already was.

Out of scope — named so it stays out

  • New maps, new coastlines, or any edit that adds/removes sea (→ more-maps plan).
  • Retrofits for First Steps, Scrimmage, Timberline, Riverline — the four no's.
  • Ports on lakes; reefs on retrofit maps (concealment stays a Straits signature for now).
  • Starting air/naval units, AI ferry planning, balance-number changes in data/ — if a retrofit only works after a stat change, the retrofit is wrong, not the stat.

9 · What shipped, and what the measurements said

All three retrofits are in and the suite is green at 436 tests. MR1–MR3 landed together because the work turned out to be smaller than drafted: no engine change, no data change, ten edited cells across three .txt files, and six new tests.

The one place the build beat the plan

The draft put Isthmus's beaches at (4,5) / (15,6), carved out of sea, and accepted a stranding risk (R3) and a ~30-tile ferry for it. Reading data/terrain/*.tres before editing changed the answer twice over. First, shoal turned out to be road plus lander access — identical costs, identical defence — so beaches could be made from coastal road and cost nothing. Second, and more useful: DropCommand only unloads from a shoal or a port, so the port is already the loading dock and a beach is only ever needed as the landing point. That flipped the placement onto the neck's two coasts, where each beach sits on the water the enemy's port opens onto — turning a five-turn circumnavigation into a two-turn assault, and deleting R3 rather than mitigating it.

Did the AI actually use any of it?

The lints prove a board is well-formed; they say nothing about whether the new facilities get used. Three 40-day AI-vs-AI matches per map, before and after, with builds and losses counted by domain — the "before" column is the same map at HEAD:

MapBeforeAfterReading
isthmus 3 of 3 unresolved at day 40; ~51 land units built, all land. Still 3 of 3 unresolved; 10–13 hulls built, 3–5 sunk per match — battleships, cruisers and submarines. The fleets are real and they fight, which is the five-tile lane working. The stalemate is pre-existing, not caused by the retrofit — worth its own look someday, out of scope here.
ironworks 3 of 3 unresolved at day 40. 2 of 3 now resolve (days 38 and 39); 13–16 aircraft built, 9–13 shot down; anti-air production jumps to 17–24. The best result of the three. Air is breaking the economy map's late-game deadlock, which is exactly the map's stated purpose extended rather than replaced.
crossfire 1 of 3 resolved (day 33). 1 of 3 resolved, sooner (day 24); 5–9 aircraft built, 3–9 shot down. Air is a minority of production and dies about as fast as it arrives. The passes game is intact — R4 resolves to keep.

No side built a lander in any run, on any map. That is the expected and correct outcome of naval plan R1 — the AI cannot plan a ferry, so it does not buy one — and it is why Isthmus's beaches are documented as a human line rather than counted as an AI capability.

Left undone, deliberately

  • Isthmus is a 40-day AI-vs-AI stalemate and was one before this work. Fixing that is a balance question about the map's economy, not about ports.
  • Short-ranged hulls on Isthmus can only fight within their own pocket without a long sail. Named here rather than smoothed away: it is what the geography says, and battleships are the answer to it.
  • The four no's stayed no. First Steps, Scrimmage, Timberline and Riverline are untouched.
