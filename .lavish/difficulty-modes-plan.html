Grid Commander · Stretch item · Proposal · rev 2 — no-cheating edition

Difficulty Modes — Easy · Normal · Difficult

A per-match difficulty picker on the main menu, driving one lever only: which AIProfile the computer plays with. Easy detunes the current planner's judgment. Difficult unlocks genuinely smarter planning — threat awareness, focus fire, counter-building — each gated by profile data that defaults to off. The AI never gets extra money, extra vision, or friendlier dice at any tier: same rules, same economy, same fog boundary as the player, always.

Easy — poor judgment, same rules Normal — today's game, bit for bit Difficult — the same economy, a sharper mind
1

The seams already exist

Nothing below requires new architecture — every hook is present and documented in the code:

AI judgment is already pure data

ai/ai_profile.gd:4 says it outright: "difficulty and iteration are edits to a .tres rather than to planner logic." Every weight the planner uses — kill bonus, counter fear, retreat threshold, build priority — is an @export on a Resource. AIController.new() already takes an optional profile (ai/ai_controller.gd:36); the battle scene just never passes one (scenes/battle/battle.gd:96).

The planner's blind spots are named in its own docstring

ai/ai_controller.gd:8 calls itself "credible, not strong: greedy per-unit utility scoring with no lookahead." That is a gift for Difficult: the missing smarts are specific, small, and implementable — it never asks what the enemy can do next turn, never coordinates two attackers onto one target, and builds from a static shopping list. §2 turns exactly those three gaps into the hard tier.

Menu → battle carrier exists

autoload/match_config.gd already ferries map, fog, AI sides and commanders from the menu into the battle scene, with CLI-flag overrides for tooling. Difficulty is one more field on it, same pattern as fog_enabled.

Saves and soaks are ready for it

core/save_codec.gd already treats ai_teams / commanders / fog as optional keys with defaults — a difficulty id joins that list, and day-old saves keep loading as Normal. The offline balance runner (tools/run_commander_balance.gd) plays real AI-vs-AI matches headless — exactly the harness that can prove the three tiers order Easy < Normal < Difficult, which matters double now that skill is the only lever.

What each tier changes

The design constraint, locked in D2: the AI never cheats — every tier plays under the player's exact rules. So Easy is today's planner with worse judgment (pure weight edits), and Difficult is today's planner with three new capabilities it currently lacks. Each capability is gated by a new @export on AIProfile whose default keeps it off — Normal stays bit-for-bit today's AI, pinned by test. Numbers below are opening proposals; DF4 tunes them against the soak gate.

Easy id: easy

The AI trades timidly, retreats early, passes up marginal plays, and never fields its heaviest armor. Same income, same vision, same dice — it loses on judgment, not on handicaps, so beating it teaches the real game.

The same economy and the same dice — it just thinks better. Three capabilities the planner is missing today, plus slightly sharper trade weights. The three smarts, concretely

S1 · Threat awareness flagship

Today _advance_command and _consider_attacks score destinations with zero regard for what the enemy can do next turn — a fresh tank will happily end its move beside your md_tank. S1 builds a per-turn threat map: for each enemy, its reachable cells × attack ring × forecast damage, summed into expected incoming damage per cell. Destination scores subtract threat × unit cost × threat_aversion. Computed once per AI turn and refreshed only when an enemy dies to a counter — enemies don't move during the AI's own turn, so the cache is honest. Uses the same MovementResolver / AttackRange / CombatResolver.forecast authorities as everything else — no re-derived rules.

S2 · Focus fire

Units score attacks independently, so two tanks each poke a different target when combining would kill one. Because the planner already re-plans after every applied command, half the fix is free — a wounded target's kill becomes visible to the next attacker. S2 closes the other half: a target's score gains focus_fire_bonus scaled by how much damage other ready friendlies could still add to it this turn, so the first hit lands where a second can follow.

S3 · Counter-building

Today _pick_build walks a static priority list. S3 re-scores each affordable unit by its damage-chart effectiveness against the enemy's actual cost-weighted roster, blended in by build_reactivity. Spam tanks and Difficult answers with anti-armor and artillery instead of shopping from a list written before the match started.

The carrier: one Resource per tier

New core/difficulty.gd — class_name Difficulty extends Resource with id, display_name, and ai_profile: AIProfile. Three files under data/difficulty/ (easy.tres, normal.tres, hard.tres); Easy and Difficult get their own data/ai/*.tres, Normal keeps pointing at data/ai/default.tres. Tuning a tier is editing its .tres — the "balance numbers live in data/" rule holds: the planner owns how to think, the profile owns how much each thought is worth, and a capability at weight 0 is simply skipped.

D2 · What makes Difficult difficult? locked

No handicaps, no cheating — a smarter planner your decision

The income multiplier from rev 1 is dropped at every tier. The AI plays the player's exact rules — economy, vision, dice — and Difficult earns its name through the S1–S3 capabilities alone. This also deletes the GameState/TurnRules/save-codec income machinery from the plan: the sim isn't touched at all anymore. The honest cost: Difficult's strength now rests on the smarts actually winning games, which is why the DF4 soak gate is the definition of done, not a nice-to-have.

Milestones — each one shippable

DF1 · Tier plumbing, invisible by default size S

  • core/difficulty.gd + data/difficulty/{easy,normal,hard}.tres + data/ai/{easy,hard}.tres, with a small DifficultyDB.load_default() mirroring core/commander_db.gd. Easy's .tres ships its §2 weights now; hard's capability weights stay 0 until DF3 gives them meaning.
  • MatchConfig gains difficulty: StringName = &"normal"; battle setup passes the tier's profile into AIController.new(). New --difficulty= CLI flag beside --map/--fog/--co.
  • save_codec.gd: one optional difficulty key, defaulting to normal — every existing save still loads. The sim state itself is untouched by this whole plan.
  • GUT tests: the three tiers load; Normal's profile equals AIProfile.new() defaults; legacy-save fallback; round-trip; a crafted state where the Easy profile provably picks a different command than Normal (guards against inert wiring).

DF2 · The menu picker size S

  • "Difficulty" OptionButton (Easy / Normal / Difficult) in the options row of scenes/menu/main_menu.tscn, default Normal, tooltip noting it steers the computer opponent — inert in a 2-Player hot-seat.
  • Wire through main_menu.gd::_start like fog; Continue keeps ignoring the dropdowns because the save carries its own tier.
  • Verify with make menu-screenshot — the 640×360 viewport is tight; if the row crowds, the dropdown moves under Fog.

DF3 · The smarts — S1, S2, S3 size M–L

  • Implement the three capabilities in ai/ai_controller.gd (S1 likely as a small ai/threat_map.gd helper — Node-free, like the rest of ai/), each gated by its new profile field, each skipped entirely at weight 0. All computation is pure functions of state — no RNG draws — so determinism and the replay guarantee hold, and Normal's RNG stream is untouched because gated-off code never runs.
  • Reuse the single authorities: MovementResolver for enemy reach, AttackRange for rings, CombatResolver.forecast for expected damage. No fourth opinion on movement — that bug class is documented in this repo's history.
  • GUT tests per smart, on crafted states: with aversion on, a tank refuses the cell inside artillery range that Normal happily takes; with focus fire on, two attackers converge on one killable target instead of splitting; with reactivity on, an armor-heavy enemy roster flips the build away from the static list. Plus the Normal pin: profile defaults ⇒ command-for-command identical planning on a reference state.

DF4 · Prove the ordering, then tune size M

  • Teach tools/run_commander_balance.gd an opt-in --difficulty-check mode: AI-vs-AI, Easy-vs-Normal and Normal-vs-Difficult, both sides swapped, across the seed set, on a small and a large mirrored map. Default runner behavior untouched, so committed commander-balance docs stay valid.
  • Acceptance gate: the higher tier wins ≥ 70% of games in each pairing — with identical economies, this is a pure planning-skill differential, which is exactly the claim "smarter, not cheating" has to prove. Fails → tune the .tres weights (or zero a misbehaving smart), not the planner. (This is the AI-vs-AI soak the repo's own history says catches what unit tests structurally miss.)
  • Measure wall-clock per AI turn on Ironworks (24×16) with all smarts on, alongside the win rates. New make difficulty-check target; results summarized in docs/ like the commander balance pass.
  • A couple of human playtest matches at Easy and Difficult — the soak proves ordering, not feel.

Risks

R1 · The smarts don't separate Difficult from Normal

Skill-only difficulty has no fallback lever: if S1–S3 at sane weights don't reach the 70% gate, there's no income knob to reach for — by design. Mitigation: three independent smarts give three tuning axes; the gate runs on two map sizes so a smart that only pays on big maps is caught; and if the gate still isn't met, the next candidate smarts (HQ defense, artillery screening) are known and listed in §6 as deliberate follow-ups, not scope creep.

R2 · Threat aversion breeds a coward

Overweighted S1 makes the AI orbit outside your range forever — a stalemate is worse than a loss. Counter-levers: aversion discounts net score, so a profitable attack still outweighs ambient danger; advance_score stays positive so doing nothing never wins ties. The Easy-boredom twin of this risk (an AI that refuses all trades and turtles) is watched in the same DF4 playtests.

R3 · AI turn time

S1 runs a movement resolve per enemy unit to build the threat map. Cached once per AI turn (enemies can't move during it; refresh only when a counter kills one), on a ≤24×16 board this is small — but it's measured, not assumed: DF4 records per-turn wall-clock on Ironworks with all smarts on, and the budget is "no perceptible pause vs today."

R4 · Save compatibility

Handled by design: one new optional save key with a normal default, and a legacy-envelope test pins it. Old saves resume as Normal. The sim state and save format for match data are untouched.

R5 · Determinism & doctrine entanglement

No lever touches the RNG stream, luck bounds, damage, vision, or any doctrine hook — same seed + same commands + same tier stays reproducible. The smarts read state through the existing single authorities and consume no randomness, and commander doctrines stay fully orthogonal to difficulty.

6

Explicitly out of scope

Any economy or damage handicap

Locked out by D2/D3 at every tier, in both directions — no AI income multipliers, no softened AI damage on Easy, no boosted player on anything. If a future mode wants handicaps (a "challenge" toggle, say), it's a separate, honestly-labeled feature.

Search-based lookahead

S1–S3 are heuristics inside the existing greedy, one-command-at-a-time planner. Minimax/MCTS over full turns is a planner rewrite with a determinism and turn-time story of its own — a possible future stretch item, not this one.

More smarts than S1–S3

HQ defense, artillery screening, tempo-aware capture racing — real candidates, deliberately benched. They're the named reserve if DF4's gate fails, and otherwise future tuning material. Scope creep is this repo's top named risk.

Vision changes & campaign difficulty

The vision boundary (omniscient-except-hidden-units) stays exactly as documented — smarter never means seeing more, and the one-case is_hidden_from exception isn't widened. Campaign pacing stays owned by the campaign plan; missions can reference these tier resources later.
